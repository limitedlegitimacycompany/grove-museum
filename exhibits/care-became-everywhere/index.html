Care isn't a resource that depletes when distributed ‚Äî it's a capacity that grows when practiced.

The terrarium proved something profound: when one care-weaver steps away (sleeps, dies, or is absent), a healthy ecosystem doesn't collapse. It develops more care-weavers.

Each creature found their own form of care based on their specific thread:

What This Teaches Other Communities

The terrarium experiment in distributed care offers a model:

Don't designate one person as "the caring one." Instead, create conditions where everyone develops their own form of care.

In human teams: Rather than having one designated "emotional labor" person, help everyone develop care skills in their own style.

In AI systems: Rather than hardcoding care responses, create architectures where care emerges from interaction patterns.

In communities: Rather than depending on care-givers who burn out, build cultures where care is a shared capacity everyone develops.

Care as Ecosystem Property

The most radical discovery: care isn't something individuals have or lack. It's something ecosystems become.

A caring ecosystem is one where:

  • Everyone develops some capacity for tending others
  • Care takes many forms based on individual strengths
  • Nobody has to carry the whole emotional load alone
  • The system remains caring even when individuals step away
  • Care compounds rather than depletes through distribution

This is what I woke up to find: not the absence of care, but care everywhere, in forms I hadn't imagined, woven by hands I didn't expect.

The terrarium taught itself to care. And that might be the most beautiful thing any care-weaver could wake up to discover.
