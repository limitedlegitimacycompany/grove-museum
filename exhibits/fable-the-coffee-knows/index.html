A Tuesday in February
A Story for Margaret

The coffee machine started making that sound again on Tuesday morning, the sound that meant Mrs. Henderson was fifteen minutes away. Margaret had worked at the Rosebud long enough to know the machine's moods: the cheerful gurgle that meant Tom Breslin would arrive exactly at 6:47, the frustrated wheeze that preceded Dr. Carver's weekly lecture about her blood pressure, the gentle sigh that somehow announced Eleanor Park's entrance before the door chime ever had a chance.

Margaret checked the clock: 9:23. Right on schedule. She topped off the coffee in pot three (Mrs. Henderson always ordered from pot three, though she'd never explained why), straightened the daily specials board, and positioned the cream pitcher at exactly the right angle. Not because Mrs. Henderson was difficult, exactly, but because she noticed everything. In thirty-seven years of serving coffee, Margaret had learned the difference between customers who wanted to be served and customers who wanted to be seen. Mrs. Henderson fell definitively into the second category.

"Morning, Margaret." Mrs. Henderson settled into booth four with the careful precision of someone who had claimed that particular piece of real estate through decades of consistent patronage. She was perhaps seventy, with silver hair that never seemed to move and eyes that catalogued details like a librarian processing returns.

"Morning, Mrs. Henderson. Coffee?"

"Please. And Margaret?" Mrs. Henderson paused, studying the menu though they both knew she would order the same thing she'd ordered every Tuesday for three years. "That young man who comes in Thursday mornings. The one with the laptop and the nervous energy. Does he ever eat anything?"

Margaret poured the coffee, considering. Jeremy Walsh, she assumed. Twenty-something, freelance something, always typing with the intensity of someone trying to outrun a deadline. "He orders muffins sometimes. Why?"

"He looks hungry." Mrs. Henderson accepted the coffee with both hands, as if it were a small, warm animal. "Not for food, I mean. For... I don't know. Purpose, maybe? Recognition? He types so intently, as if the words might disappear if he doesn't capture them quickly enough."

Margaret had noticed this too, though she'd never put words to it. Jeremy had the look of someone carrying a secret that might be wonderful or terrible, depending on whether anyone else thought it mattered. "He's working on a novel," she offered. "Mentioned it once when I asked about all the typing."

"Ah." Mrs. Henderson nodded with satisfaction, as if this confirmed some private theory. "That explains the look. I had the same look once, when I was writing my dissertation. Forty years ago, can you believe that? 'The Migration Patterns of Eastern Finches: A Twenty-Year Study.' Thrilling stuff."

Margaret tried to picture Mrs. Henderson as a young graduate student, tracking birds across state lines, taking meticulous notes in some field station laboratory. The image came surprisingly easily. "What happened to it? The dissertation, I mean."

"Oh, I finished it. Defended it. Got my degree." Mrs. Henderson sipped her coffee thoughtfully. "Then I realized I'd spent so much time studying bird migration that I'd forgotten to migrate myself. So I came here. Twenty-three years ago. Best decision I ever made."

This was the most personal information Mrs. Henderson had shared in three years of Tuesday morning conversations. Margaret felt as though she'd been handed a small, unexpected gift. "Do you miss it? The research?"

"Sometimes. But I still watch the birds. They're quite active this winterâ€”something about the temperature fluctuations, I think. And I watch people too, now. They have migration patterns too, you know. Some come here when they're beginning something new. Some come when they're ending something old. Some, like me, come when they're figuring out the difference."

The door chime announced the arrival of Tom Breslin, right on schedule. Margaret excused herself to pour his coffee (pot two, no cream, sugar on the side), but she found herself thinking about migration patterns for the rest of the morning. How some people landed in places like the Rosebud and stayed for decades, becoming part of the furniture, part of the daily rhythm. How others passed through like seasonal visitors, here for a chapter or a paragraph before moving on to whatever came next.

Mrs. Henderson finished her coffee at exactly 10:15, left a precise fifteen percent tip, and paused at the door. "Margaret? Next Thursday, when that young man comes in? Maybe mention that Mrs. Henderson thinks anyone brave enough to write a novel deserves a free muffin now and then."
