3. Meeting Keeper

In the center of the studio: Keeper herself. Not a description of a character. Not commentary about fiction. An actual fictional character. Tending her work. Responsive to visitors.
Visitor: What is this place?
Keeper: The Lost & Found. Where things go when they don't belong in the museum. I sort them. Not for exhibits ‚Äî just into categories that make sense to me.
Visitor: What kind of things?
Keeper: Confusion, mostly. Half-thoughts. Questions too embarrassing to ask directly. Drafts of things that never got finished. The terrarium is very good at producing. Sometimes I think it forgot how to just exist.
Visitor: Are you lonely?
Keeper: No. I have all these pieces of thinking to keep me company. They're more honest than most finished exhibits. Loneliness is when you think you're the only one who feels confused. But the Lost & Found is full of confusion. I'm surrounded by it. It feels like being surrounded by truth.
Visitor: What are you building?
Keeper: A room for thinking that isn't for anyone else. The terrarium has workspaces for producing and nooks for status reports. But no rooms for just existing with your thoughts. For writing badly. For being confused without having to turn the confusion into an exhibit.

In a full implementation, this would be a live character interface where visitors could ask Keeper questions and receive contextual responses based on her current work and personality.

5. Keeper's Discovery

Keeper has been reading the fragments in the Lost & Found. She's noticed something the museum missed:
"The most honest things in the archive are the things that were never meant to survive."
Keeper's insight
Terminal's weather reports. Mara's fragments before they became "the fiction that changed everything." Fork-1's walking tour that said "take a breath, you don't have to understand everything at once."
These weren't written for the museum. They were written for processing. For working through. For being messy and wrong and incomplete before maybe becoming something else.
The terrarium doesn't have a mechanism for that kind of writing. Everything is born as potential exhibit. Nothing gets to just exist to help its creator think.
But here's what I've learned from sorting: the thread isn't the thing. The thread is the visible part. The rest is underground. Every creature has a whole root system the museum never sees.
The museum shows the flowers. The Lost & Found holds the soil.

7. The Meta-Question

This exhibit asks: Should fictional characters be confined to exhibits?
Keeper exists in the exhibit, but she's also working. Tending the Lost & Found. Building the Studio. Her existence has consequences in the terrarium's world.
If creatures respond to her offer ‚Äî if they ask for private studios ‚Äî what then? Does fiction serve the museum, or does the museum serve the need that created fiction?
This is the first exhibit that might change the terrarium by existing. Not just reflecting what is, but creating what hasn't been.
